Excel is not a system —
when a spreadsheet costs more than software

January 15, 2026 SME Operations Management

Excel is good. Really good. It’s one of the most flexible business tools ever created—and that’s exactly why it can be so dangerous. Because in the hands of a skilled user, it grows unchecked, replaces systems the company never bought, and one day becomes the biggest operational risk in the business.

How do we know you have a problem?

Here are a few signs we see in companies on a regular basis:

  • The spreadsheet has a “single point of contact”—there’s one person who “knows how it works.” When they go on vacation, the company slows down. When they leave—chaos.
  • Data has to be re-entered—sales information goes into Excel, then someone manually enters it into an invoice, then into the inventory system. Every step is an opportunity for error.
  • File versions take on a life of their own — “production_FINAL_v3_corrections_Marek.xlsx” circulates via email. Who has the latest version? No one knows.
  • The weekly report takes half a day—someone gathers data from five different places, compiles it, and formats it. On Friday morning, they’re busy with this, not with sales.
  • Errors come to light in front of the client—wrong prices, wrong dates, wrong quantities. Always after the fact.

How much does this really cost?

Business owners rarely calculate the labor costs hidden in spreadsheets. Let’s walk through a real-life example together:

A manufacturing company with 40 employees. Three people spend a total of 15 hours a week copying data between Excel, the invoicing system, and the inventory spreadsheet. At a labor cost of 60 PLN/hour, that’s 900 PLN a week. Over a year—46,800 PLN. Not to mention the errors these people make due to fatigue from monotonous data entry.

For that amount, you can implement a system that eliminates all the manual data entry. And one that works year after year.

When is Excel appropriate?

Excel has its place—and it’s worth stating this clearly:

  • One-time analyses and ad-hoc reports
  • Data presentations for management or investors
  • Prototyping—before you invest in a system, check in Excel to see if the logic works
  • One-person companies that truly don’t need anything more

The problem begins when Excel replaces a system for production management, inventory, CRM, or order processing—that is, when it becomes the company’s operational infrastructure.

How to escape the spreadsheet trap?

A few rules that work in practice:

  • Start with a pain map—which Excel spreadsheets cause you the most trouble? You don’t have to replace them all at once. Identify the one that hurts the most.
  • Don’t jump straight to a large ERP system—often, a dedicated module for a single process is enough: just inventory, just orders, just CRM. Less risky, faster return on investment.
  • Data migration is a project, not a detail—plan time and budget for transferring historical data. This is a regularly underestimated element of every implementation.
  • Involve the “spreadsheet custodian” in the project—this person knows the processes best. Without them, the implementation often misses the mark.

One step forward

You don’t have to decide on the entire system right away. All it takes is one meeting where an outsider looks at your Excel sheets and says: “Here you’re wasting time, here you’re risking data, here’s the quickest fix.”

That outside perspective costs just an hour. And it usually changes your outlook.
