AI-powered warehouse automation —
first steps

March 1, 2026 AI Warehouse WMS

A warehouse in a manufacturing company is one of those places where chaos is almost inevitable. Thousands of items, receipts, shipments, transfers, inventories, shortages, surpluses — everything happens quickly and simultaneously. AI doesn’t solve this problem with a single click, but in specific areas it can make a huge difference. Where to start?

The most common warehouse pain points without AI

Before we discuss solutions, let’s identify the problems we see most often:

  • Inventory levels don’t match reality—the system says 200 units, but there are 147 on the shelf. An annual inventory can’t keep up with the pace of operations.
  • Replenishment orders are placed too late or too early—someone realizes a raw material is missing only when the production line stops. Or the opposite—the warehouse is overloaded because someone “ordered with a buffer.”
  • Order picking takes too long—employees walk around the warehouse without an optimal route, looking for goods in the wrong places.
  • Paper or Excel documentation—WZs, PZs, and MM forms are entered manually, often with delays or errors.

What can AI change in the warehouse?

1. Demand forecasting

This is one of the biggest benefits of AI in the warehouse. The model analyzes sales history, seasonality, and current orders in progress to forecast what will be needed and when. It doesn’t replace human decision-making—it provides data that would be impossible to collect manually.

Result: fewer downtimes due to stockouts, lower inventory levels (because you know when to reorder, so you don’t need to keep a large buffer “just in case”).

2. Automatic Reorder Alerts

The AI agent monitors inventory levels in real time and, upon reaching the reorder point (which it optimizes itself based on historical data), generates a reorder proposal for approval. The warehouse manager receives a notification: “I propose ordering 500 units of Product X from Supplier Y—lead time 5 days, current stock will last for 6 days of production.” One click, order sent.

3. Order Picking Optimization

AI-powered WMS systems calculate the optimal picking route for each order or group of orders. The employee receives a detailed list on their terminal (or phone) with the order of items arranged to minimize the distance traveled. In large warehouses, this reduces picking time by 20–35%.

4. Anomaly detection

AI analyzes patterns: if a product suddenly starts selling three times faster than usual, the system alerts you before a shortage occurs. If an employee consistently makes mistakes during receiving—this is visible in the data, allowing you to respond before the problem escalates.

Where to start in practice?

Not by buying an expensive WMS. Seriously.

The first step is usually digitizing basic operations: every receipt and issue should be recorded electronically, preferably with a barcode or QR code. Without reliable input data, AI has nothing to work with.

If you already have a WMS or an ERP with a warehouse module—check if it collects historical data and if you can export it. This is the starting point for your first predictive model.

If you’re starting from scratch—plan to implement a small WMS (costing 10,000–30,000 PLN for a small warehouse) before implementing AI. The order matters.

How much does it cost and when will you see a return on investment?

An AI module for forecasting and automatic replenishment signals as an extension of an existing system: PLN 15,000–40,000 for implementation. For a warehouse worth PLN 500,000 with an 8x annual turnover—even a 5% improvement in efficiency amounts to PLN 40,000 per year. Payback in one year.
