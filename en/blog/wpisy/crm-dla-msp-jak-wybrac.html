CRM for SMEs — how to choose it (and what not to buy)

July 1, 2026 CRM SME Choosing a System

The owner of a 15-person trading company once told us: "I bought a CRM because everyone said we needed one. Six months later my sales reps were still working from notes on their phones, and I was paying €200 a month for something nobody logged into." That's not the exception — it's the most common scenario we see. The problem is almost never that a company chose the wrong CRM. It's that it chose a CRM before it knew what it needed one for.

This article is here to help you make that decision deliberately: what a CRM actually does, what your options are, and — just as important — what you'd be better off not buying.

What you need a CRM for (and definitely what you don't)

At its simplest, a CRM is one place where you know: who your customer is, what stage the conversation is at, and what needs to happen next. It doesn't need to do anything more than that to start. If that knowledge currently lives in your sales reps' heads, in emails, and in spreadsheets, a CRM makes sense.

What a CRM is not, however, is an invoicing system, a production management tool, or a warehouse system. Companies often try to buy "one system to do everything" — and that's where the trouble starts. A few signs you genuinely need a CRM:

  • A sales rep leaves the company and takes the entire customer contact history with them.
  • You don't know how many open quotes you have or how many are "hot."
  • A customer calls and nobody remembers what was discussed a month ago.
  • Your sales forecast for next quarter is basically a guess.

Three paths — and who each one works for

You essentially have three approaches, each with a different cost and risk profile.

1. Off-the-shelf SaaS (subscription-based, ready-made solutions). You're up and running in days, paying monthly per user — realistically somewhere between €12 and €35 per person. Ideal when your sales process is fairly standard: lead, quote, negotiation, close. The downside: the more your business deviates from "typical," the more things you'll have to bend to fit the system instead of the other way around.

2. A custom-built solution. A system written around your specific process — for example, when you sell made-to-order production and every quote involves 30 parameters. The entry cost is higher (from a few thousand up to several tens of thousands of euros), but you're not paying per user forever, and the system fits like a glove. This makes sense when the CRM is the heart of your business, not an add-on.

3. Customizing an open-source platform. You take a free engine and adapt it. The appeal is the "zero" license cost, but the real cost is implementation, hosting, and maintenance. This works for companies with in-house technical capacity or a steady IT partner — otherwise you can easily end up with a system nobody knows how to update.

Why big systems get in the way of small companies

The biggest trap for SMEs is buying a CRM that's "too big for you" — a behemoth designed for a corporation with a sales department of hundreds of people. It looks impressive in the demo, and then it turns out that:

  • The rollout takes months and requires a certified consultant billing several hundred euros an hour.
  • Sales reps have to fill in 12 fields just to log one phone call — so they stop logging calls.
  • You're paying for 200 features and using six of them.

A simple rule: a system nobody logs into is worth zero, no matter the price. A modest CRM your team actually uses beats a powerful one that sits empty.

What not to buy

A few things that, in a small company, almost always turn out to be a waste of money: modules you can't describe in a single sentence; multi-year contracts with no trial period; "premium" packages bought at launch, before you even know how your team uses the basics. Start with the minimum and expand only when something actually starts to hurt.

How to choose — in practice

  1. Write down your sales process in 4–6 steps. This is the single most important document in this decision.
  2. List 3 specific things you want the CRM to give you — concretely, e.g., "see all open quotes above €2,000."
  3. Test 2 solutions with real data, not a demo. Give them to your sales reps for two weeks.
  4. Check how easily you can export your data if things don't work out. Not having that option is a red flag.

Choosing the right CRM isn't about finding the "best" system on the market — it's about matching the tool to how your company actually sells. Sometimes that's a ready-made subscription for €70 a month. Sometimes it's something built to measure. The key is to start from the process, not the price list.
