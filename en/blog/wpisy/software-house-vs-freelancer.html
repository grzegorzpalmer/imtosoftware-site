Software house vs. freelancer — what to choose when commissioning custom software?

May 15, 2026 Software House Commissioning a System SME

The decision only looks simple at first glance. A freelancer quotes €4,000, a software house quotes €15,000. You look at both numbers and think: what am I paying so much more for? Six months later it turns out the answer wasn't in the price — it was in whether the project would actually get finished at all.

We work with companies that came to us after a failed collaboration with a freelancer — and with companies for whom a freelancer was entirely sufficient. The difference isn't in the quality of the people. It's in the kind of project they were handed.

When a freelancer is the better choice

There are situations where a software house is overpaying for what you need. If your project matches any of the following traits, a freelancer is probably enough:

  • A small, well-defined scope. A product configurator for your website, a simple calculator for the sales team, an extra report added to an existing Power BI dashboard. Something you can describe on a single page and that doesn't require changes in five places at once.
  • One technology, one environment. A WordPress site, an Excel macro, a Python script to process files. The task doesn't require coordinating between backend, frontend, database, and integrations.
  • A short time horizon. Something that needs to be built in 2–4 weeks and then just runs on its own. No development roadmap, no new modules planned every quarter.
  • Tolerance for risk. If the project slips by a month, or has to restart with a different contractor, it won't bring the company to a halt. It's nice-to-have, not a critical part of operations.

A real example: a client recently needed a tool that pulls data from a supplier's API once a day and sends a summary email. Three days of work, €1,100, and the freelancer did a great job. A software house would have quoted €4,000 with analysis, documentation, and testing — completely unnecessary for a task like that.

When you need a software house

The situation changes dramatically once a project touches several parts of the business at once. That's when a single person — even a very good one — becomes a bottleneck.

  • The system touches more than one department. Sales, warehouse, and accounting all need to see the same data — each with a different access level, in a different format. A freelancer can handle one department well; tying three together requires someone who thinks in architecture, not just code.
  • Integration with existing systems. ERP, CRM, warehouse management, an online marketplace, a wholesale platform — every one of these connections is a potential minefield. It takes experience and, more importantly, someone who will maintain those integrations when a vendor changes its API.
  • The project grows with the company. 30 users today, 80 next year, a branch office in Germany the year after. The system needs to be designed for scale from day one. A freelancer builds for "works today"; a software house builds for "works in three years."
  • Critical business processes. If the system goes down and the company grinds to a halt, you need a team that can bring it back up within the hour — not someone who happens to be on vacation.

Three signs a freelancer isn't enough anymore — even if they were the right call at first

Sometimes starting with a freelancer is the right call, but the project outgrows them. When you notice these symptoms, it's time for a serious conversation:

  • "I'll get to it soon" has been going on for three months. A freelancer typically juggles 2–4 clients at once. Your priority depends on who shouted loudest most recently. A software house has that priority written into the contract and the SLA.
  • They disappeared for two weeks and aren't answering. It's rarely bad faith — illness, an accident, a bigger project for another client. A one-person contractor means one-person availability.
  • There's no one to take over the project. The code exists only in the freelancer's head. No documentation, no tests, no second person who understands the structure. If they leave, you start from zero.

What to check when choosing — regardless of who you hire

A few questions worth asking any contractor before you sign anything:

  • What happens to the code when we end the engagement? Do I get the full repository, documentation, and access credentials? Will someone else be able to take it over?
  • What does support look like after go-live? A free first month is standard. The real question is: what happens after that, during what hours, and with what response time?
  • Show me three similar projects. Not a portfolio homepage — specific cases in my industry or at a similar scale. If someone has no references you can actually call, that's a warning sign.
  • What won't be done for this price? Every good quote has a clear "in scope" and "out of scope" list. If everything is "included," brace for unpleasant surprises along the way.

The most common mistake: cutting costs in the wrong place

The worst thing you can do is choose a freelancer for a project that needs a team — because the quote is 60% lower. A year later it turns out everything has to be rebuilt from scratch, and the actual cost ends up double the software house's original quote.

On the flip side — bringing in a team of six for a three-week script is just as much of a mismatch. In that case you're paying not for code, but for a company structure your project doesn't need.

The simplest rule we use in conversations with clients: if you're afraid the project will get stuck depending on a single person, you need a team. If you're afraid a team will bill you for things you don't actually need, one good contractor is enough.
