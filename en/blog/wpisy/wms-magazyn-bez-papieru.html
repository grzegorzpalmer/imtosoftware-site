WMS — How Warehouse Digitization
Eliminates Errors and Speeds Up Shipments

April 15, 2026 WMS Warehouse Logistics

A warehouse without a system is a warehouse managed by memory and paper. Someone knows where everything is—but only until they go on vacation. Someone else knows how much inventory we have—but only until someone takes something without logging it. Companies operate this way for years and think, “It works somehow.” Until it stops working—and then they look for a WMS.

This article explains: what a WMS is, what problems it solves, when it makes financial sense, and where to start. No logistics jargon.

What is a WMS—in one sentence

A WMS (Warehouse Management System) is a system that knows: what you have in the warehouse, where it’s located, where it came from, and where it’s going next. Every movement of goods—receipt, transfer, picking, shipment—is recorded in real time, usually via a scanner or tablet.

Sounds simple. In practice, it’s a change that eliminates several completely unnecessary problems that most companies don’t even consider problems—because they’re so old they’ve become “the norm.”

Five problems that WMS eliminates immediately

1. Inventory discrepancies. Once a year we take an inventory, and it turns out that “on paper” there are 340 items, but in reality there are 291. Where does the difference come from? No one knows. In companies without a WMS, discrepancies of 5–15% in inventory levels are the norm. With a WMS—less than 0.5%.

2. Time spent searching for goods. “Where are those M8 screws?” “Check shelf C, or maybe D—Marek was rearranging things.” A warehouse worker spends an average of 15–20 minutes a day searching for goods that aren’t in the system. In a 10-person warehouse, that’s over 2,500 hours a year—wasted.

3. Picking errors. The customer receives something other than what they ordered. Or they receive everything correctly, but in the wrong quantity. Complaints, returns, reshipments—the cost of a single mistake is often 80–200 PLN (shipping, employee time, loss of customer trust). A WMS with scan verification reduces picking errors by over 90%.

4. Lack of real-time availability information. A sales representative promises a customer delivery on Thursday. They don’t know that the item is no longer in stock. They find out on Wednesday. Situations like this damage customer relationships faster than competitors’ prices.

5. Reliance on specific individuals. When Marek takes time off, no one knows where to get the goods for orders 4000–4200. Knowledge about warehouse organization is in people’s heads, not in the system. A WMS transfers this knowledge from people’s heads to a database.

What a WMS does in practice — step by step

Imagine receiving a delivery at a company with a WMS:

  1. The driver arrives with the goods. The warehouse worker scans the barcodes on the pallets.
  2. The system automatically compares the delivery with the purchase order—verifying quantities and product range.
  3. The system indicates which rack to place each item on (based on an algorithm: rotation, weight, category).
  4. Inventory levels are updated in real time—sales staff can see availability immediately.

The same applies to shipping: the system generates a picking list with the optimal route through the warehouse (pick path), the warehouse worker scans each item picked, and errors are detected before the package goes onto the pallet—not at the customer’s end.

In the SKI19 project—a WMS system for a textile manufacturer—the picking time for a single order was reduced from 18 to 7 minutes. With 60 orders per day, that’s over 3 hours saved every day.

When a WMS Makes Financial Sense

A simple calculation: if your warehouse handles at least 20–30 orders per day or employs 3+ warehouse workers, a WMS typically pays for itself within 12–18 months. Here’s where that return comes from:

  • Reduced picking errors — fewer complaints, fewer returns, fewer reshipments
  • Shorter picking times — the same number of orders handled by fewer people or faster
  • Lower safety stock — when you know exactly what you have, you don’t need to keep a 30% surplus “just in case”
  • Better inventory turnover — the system enforces the FIFO (first in, first out) principle, which reduces obsolescence and write-offs

Companies with fewer than 15 orders per day typically do not need a full WMS—an ERP warehouse module or a well-configured spreadsheet with periodic inventory counts is sufficient.

WMS as a standalone system or as an ERP module?

This is the question we hear most often. The answer depends on the scale:

WMS module within an ERP system (e.g., Comarch, Optima, SAP)—sufficient for most SMEs. All data in one place, easier integration with purchasing and sales, lower implementation costs. Limitations only become apparent with very high volumes or specific requirements (e.g., cold storage zone management).

Dedicated WMS — makes sense when the warehouse is the operational hub of the business: high volumes, multiple locations, integrations with couriers and e-commerce platforms, advanced picking algorithms. Higher cost, but also incomparably greater capabilities.

For a manufacturing company employing 20–100 people and handling up to several hundred orders a day — a WMS module within a well-implemented ERP system is sufficient in 90% of cases.

Where to start

Not with choosing a system. Start by identifying the pain points. Before you start talking to vendors, answer three questions:

  • How many times a month does the wrong item or the wrong quantity reach the customer?
  • How much time do employees spend each day searching for goods or resolving discrepancies?
  • How significant were the discrepancies in the last inventory?

If you have specific numbers, your conversation with a WMS provider will be completely different. Instead of buying a “warehouse system,” you’re buying a solution to a specific problem with a specific value. And you can assess whether the offer makes sense—before you sign the contract.
