IoT in manufacturing — how to collect machine data without a big budget

June 15, 2026 IoT Manufacturing Data Automation

When you hear "IoT" or "Industry 4.0," you probably picture a massive factory, hundreds of sensors, and a budget a small company simply doesn't have. That misconception costs manufacturing companies real money — because it discourages them from taking the first, cheap step. And that first step is exactly the one with the biggest payoff.

IoT (the Internet of Things) is, in practice, simply collecting data from machines and automatically sending it to one place where you can see it. No walking the shop floor, no asking the shift supervisor, no copying numbers from a piece of paper into Excel. In this article we show how to start sensibly — with a few hundred euros, not a few hundred thousand.

Why bother collecting data from machines at all?

Picture a plant running three CNC machines. The owner senses that "something's causing downtime" but doesn't know exactly how much. After connecting a simple counter, it turns out the machines are actually running only 58% of the shift — the rest is changeovers, waiting for material, and micro-stoppages nobody had ever counted. Only that data made it possible to talk in specifics instead of impressions.

The most common things worth monitoring at the start:

  • Machine run time — how much it's actually producing versus standing idle. This is the basis of the OEE metric (overall equipment effectiveness).
  • Piece / cycle count — automatic counting of output instead of a manual report at the end of the shift.
  • Downtime and its causes — when a machine stops and why (breakdown, missing material, changeover).
  • Process parameters — temperature, pressure, vibration, if they affect product quality.

How to do it without a big budget

The key is to start with one machine and one question you want an answer to — not with digitizing the whole plant at once. Here's a sensible path:

  1. Check what the machine is already "telling" you. Many newer devices have ports (e.g., Modbus, OPC UA) or simple signal outputs. Sometimes the data is right there — you just have to read it.
  2. For older machines — a cheap external sensor. A current sensor, a vibration sensor, or even a basic proximity sensor counting movement can deliver 80% of the insight you need for a few hundred euros per station.
  3. A small computer as a "go-between." A device the size of a matchbox (e.g., a Raspberry Pi–class board or an industrial controller) collects the signals and sends them to the cloud or a local server.
  4. A simple dashboard. The data lands on a screen you can check from your phone or computer — no need to log into the machine itself.

A pilot on one or two machines usually comes in at a few thousand euros and a few weeks of work. That's a cost comparable to a single extended downtime event that the data would let you avoid.

From data to decisions — because numbers alone aren't enough

Collecting the data is only half the job. The other half is turning numbers into decisions. A dashboard full of charts that nobody looks at is worth about as much as a binder full of paper. That's why, from the start, it's worth asking: "what will we do differently once we see this number?"

Examples of real results at smaller manufacturers:

  • A downtime alert sent to the shift manager's phone when a machine stands idle for more than 10 minutes — a reaction within minutes instead of after the shift ends.
  • An automatic production report in the inbox every morning, instead of chasing shift supervisors for data.
  • Detecting a creeping failure — rising motor vibration signals a fault before the machine actually breaks down.

This is exactly where automation — and, increasingly, AI — comes in: the system doesn't just display data, it flags what's unusual on its own and suggests where to look for savings.

What to avoid at the start

The most common mistakes we see in companies starting their IoT journey are easy to avoid. Don't buy a comprehensive "do everything" system before checking whether you'll even look at the data. Don't connect twenty machines at once — with just one, you'll learn more, and more cheaply. And don't leave the project without an owner: someone in the company has to be responsible for making sure the data actually changes decisions, not just looks nice on a screen.

IoT in manufacturing isn't a luxury reserved for corporations. Today it's one of the cheapest tools a small company has to regain control over what's happening on the shop floor — as long as you start with one small, concrete question.
