How much does it really cost to implement ERP
in a small manufacturing company?

December 15, 2025 ERP Costs SMEs

“ERP implementation costs as much as a small country’s defense budget”—this belief has been circulating among owners of small manufacturing companies for years. And for good reason. Huge systems for corporations, massive projects, hefty invoices. But does it have to be this way for a company employing 20–80 people?

We’re breaking it down to the basics—no beating around the bush.

What goes into the “cost of ERP”?

Most business owners think about the cost of the license. That’s a mistake—the license is often the smaller part of the expense. The true cost of implementation consists of four elements:

  • License or access fee — in SaaS models, this is a monthly subscription per user (typically 50–200 PLN/month per person). In dedicated systems, you pay once for the software.
  • Implementation and configuration—process analysis, system configuration tailored to the company, data migration. This often accounts for 60–70% of the total project cost.
  • Training — employees must learn how to use the new tool. An underestimated element that can be the source of the greatest delays.
  • Maintenance and support — monthly cost of technical support, updates, and troubleshooting. Often overlooked during the budgeting phase.

How much does this cost in numbers?

For a small manufacturing company (20–50 employees, 5–15 system users), we can talk about three categories:

  • Off-the-shelf SaaS (e.g., Comarch ERP Optima, Symfonia) — implementation: 15,000–40,000 PLN + subscription: 500–2,000 PLN/month. Quick start, but configuration limitations.
  • Semi-dedicated system (heavily customized off-the-shelf solution) — implementation: PLN 40,000–100,000 + maintenance. Better tailored, but still a compromise.
  • Fully dedicated system — starting at 80,000 PLN and up, with no upper limit depending on complexity. More expensive upfront, cheaper in the long run, fully tailored to your processes.

Where is the break-even point? Simply put: if your employees collectively lose more than 30–40 hours a week on manual data entry, copying between spreadsheets, and searching for information—the system pays for itself in 12–24 months, even with a higher implementation budget.

Pitfalls Not Mentioned in the Proposal

Implementation companies rarely speak openly about several costs that regularly surprise clients:

  • Data migration — transferring sales history, inventory levels, and the customer database from Excel or an old system. This can cost as much as the implementation itself.
  • Integrations with other systems — connecting the ERP to an e-commerce system, invoicing software, and warehouse scanners. Each integration requires an additional budget.
  • Your team’s time — implementation involves not only the IT company but also your employees. Weeks of meetings, testing, and data entry. This is a real cost, though it doesn’t appear on the invoice.
  • Changes during the project — “Let’s add this too...” This is the most common phrase in IT projects and the quickest way to go over budget.

How to avoid overspending?

A few rules that work:

  • Before you start talking to vendors, map out your processes. You know what you want to change—you can assess whether the proposal addresses it.
  • Negotiate the scope, not the price. It’s better to implement less for a reasonable price than to overpay for features you won’t use.
  • Request references from companies of a similar size and industry. “We’ve implemented it at a large corporation” doesn’t mean they can handle a 30-person production team.
  • Plan a budget for the “post-implementation” phase—the first 3 months of use will generate requests for improvements. Good technology partners stick around and respond.

Summary

Implementing ERP in a small manufacturing company doesn’t have to mean a multi-million-dollar project. The key is to clearly define what you really want to change—and to choose a partner who understands your processes, not just their product catalog.

Before signing a contract, it’s worth talking to someone who has implemented systems in companies similar to yours. A 15-minute conversation can save you months of frustration.
